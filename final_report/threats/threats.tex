\chapter{Threats to validity}
\label{ch:threats}

This chapter collects the limitations mentioned throughout Chapter~\ref{ch:results} and discusses design choices that affect the validity of our claims. We organize the discussion using the standard four-axis framework from empirical software engineering~\cite{wohlin2012experimentation}: construct validity, internal validity, external validity, and statistical conclusion validity. For each of the four empirical studies, we identify specific threats, discuss implementation-side concerns, and note what falls out of scope. We first cover the mitigations already built into the design, then discuss the remaining open limitations.

\section{Mitigations and design choices}
\label{sec:threats-mitigations}

Several threats were addressed at design time. The remaining sections of this chapter should be read against the following mitigations.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Three-way inter-rater reliability.} Two participants (referred to as P1 and P2 throughout Chapter~\ref{ch:results}) labelled all $45$ injection sessions independently of the author and before either of them performed a user-study session of their own. Cohen's $\kappa$ across the three rater pairs is reported per kind in \S\ref{sec:results-kappa}, and the author-vs-rater values are visible alongside the rater-vs-rater pair so any author self-bias is on the page.
    \item \textbf{Multi-recorder sessions on a disjoint fixture.} The user study collects six sessions each from P1 and P2 on an order-processing fixture that is structurally separate from the library fixture used for the injection sessions, so the user-study numbers are not contaminated by familiarity with the injection sessions.
    \item \textbf{Deterministic tie-break across rankings.} Every divergence-point ranking in this thesis uses an explicit \texttt{compareByDescending\{magnitude\}.thenBy\{stepIndex\}} secondary key. Without this, equal-magnitude divergence points (notably the many \texttt{HYGIENE} \texttt{COMMIT\_GAP} points that all carry magnitude exactly $W_{\textsc{cg}}$) would sort by insertion order, which is non-deterministic under weight perturbation and would produce spurious ranking changes in the sensitivity sweep.
    \item \textbf{Multi-knob robustness on top of single-knob sensitivity.} The sensitivity sweep (\S\ref{sec:results-sensitivity}) is combined with a multi-knob Monte Carlo experiment that perturbs every weight simultaneously from a log-normal distribution. The headline robustness numbers are reported against both designs rather than the single-knob design alone.
    \item \textbf{Honest score-floor disclosure.} The IDE-replay cases where the suggested alternative doesn't improve the score, reported in \S\ref{sec:results-divergence}, are attributed in-line to the $[0, 100]$ score clamp rather than to a miscalibrated \texttt{manualIde} penalty. Three of the four misses are sessions where the user's score was already at the floor and the alternative could not score lower.
    \item \textbf{Validator-check disclosure.} The ORDERING recall gap ($0.36$ on any-expected) is documented as a deliberate consequence of the reorder synthesiser's validator check, which excludes windows containing steps the synthesiser engine cannot reproduce. The recall gap is not papered over.
    \item \textbf{Reproducible source for every reported number.} Every table in Chapter~\ref{ch:results} is generated by a notebook cell carrying an explicit markdown comment naming the chapter table it reproduces (\texttt{tool/notebooks/experiments.ipynb}). The chapter's numbers are recomputable from the saved analysis-report JSONs.
    \item \textbf{Per-session-not-per-step true-positive policy disclosed.} The kind classifier counts a true positive when a divergence point of the expected kind fires anywhere in the session, not strictly at the playbook's \texttt{target\_step}. This is named in \S\ref{sec:results-divergence}'s caveats with the reason (step indices are a function of the plugin's edit-burst-flush cadence and are not semantically stable across recordings) rather than left undocumented.
\end{itemize}

The remaining threats fall under four validity axes plus the per-experiment subsection.

\section{Construct validity}
\label{sec:threats-construct}

\paragraph{There is no external ground truth for ``true refactoring quality''.}
The process-quality score $J$ measures process behaviours the literature identifies as bad practice: leaving the build broken, going long stretches without committing, refactoring manually where an IDE refactor would have worked, and so on. It does not predict bug rates, time-to-merge, or any other downstream-of-refactoring outcome, and Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} does not claim it does. Calibrating a formula like this against a held-out outcome dataset would require a dataset linking session-level process signals to those outcomes, and we are not aware of one that exists. The weights are grounded in the literature-supported severity orderings of \S\ref{sec:methodology-weights} rather than fitted to data; the chapter addresses robustness and inter-rater agreement empirically, but not predictive validity.

\paragraph{The score is not a maintainability index.}
Snapshot composites like the Maintainability Index~\cite{coleman1994maintainability} and QMOOD~\cite{bansiya2002qmood} were regression-fitted against expert ratings of completed code. The score in this thesis is trajectory-level and process-side; it asks ``did the refactoring follow good practice on the way there'', not ``is the code good now''. The two are different constructs and the chapter does not conflate them.

\paragraph{The four-kind taxonomy is a methodology choice, not a discovered structure.}
The divergence-point kinds (\texttt{IDE\_REPLAY}, \texttt{REWORK}, \texttt{HYGIENE}, \texttt{ORDERING}) were chosen for the rater-design rationale set out in \S\ref{sec:methodology-rater}: they are operationally distinguishable behaviours the literature flags as bad practice, and they admit a synthesiser that can produce a concrete alternative for each. A different taxonomy is defensible. Collapsing \texttt{HYGIENE} into separate top-level \texttt{TESTS\_SKIPPED} and \texttt{COMMIT\_GAP} kinds, or merging \texttt{REWORK} into \texttt{ORDERING} on the grounds that both are about wasted steps, would change the precision/recall numbers without changing the chapter's headline claims.

\paragraph{\texttt{SuboptimalOrdering} is author-claimed, not measured.}
The five ordering-injection sessions in the playbook carry the \texttt{SuboptimalOrdering} pattern label because the playbook author selected an ordering they believed was worse than its alternatives. The score formula cannot independently verify this: \S\ref{sec:results-headline} reports exactly this finding ($0$ of the $5$ ``suboptimal'' orderings produce a positive-magnitude reorder alternative under the production formula, because the formula and the synthesiser together treat commuting orderings as observationally equivalent). The label reflects what we designed the session to contain, not objective ground truth, so interpret the precision/recall numbers with that in mind.

\paragraph{The AST-equivalence audit uses an informal definition.}
A candidate permutation is accepted by the reorder synthesiser only if its terminal AST is equivalent to the user's. Equivalence is defined as ``modulo syntactic differences that do not change behaviour'' (\S\ref{sec:methodology-reorder}) and implemented using an AST hash. The exact set of differences the hash treats as behaviour-preserving (e.g.\ comment placement, annotation order, whitespace) is implicit in the hash implementation rather than spelled out in the chapter. A hash that is too loose would admit reorders that change behaviour and a hash that is too tight would reject reorders that are genuinely equivalent. It catches the obvious failures, but the exact boundary is a design choice the chapter doesn't formally specify.

\section{Internal validity}
\label{sec:threats-internal}

\paragraph{The single-knob sensitivity sweep is combined with a multi-knob design rather than replaced.}
The headline top-$1$ stability number in \S\ref{sec:results-sensitivity} is computed under single-knob perturbation: one weight changes per row, the rest stay at production. Real misspecification of weights rarely happens one at a time, so a multi-knob Monte Carlo experiment runs alongside (every weight perturbed simultaneously from $\exp(\sigma \cdot \mathcal{N}(0, 1))$ with $\sigma = \ln 2$). Top-$1$ stability falls from $95.0\%$ under single-knob to $77.0\%$ under multi-knob on the user-study sessions. The formula is robust to plausibly-sized perturbations of any single weight, and robust to roughly four-in-five plausibly-sized simultaneous perturbations of all weights, but is not robust to arbitrarily-large simultaneous moves.

\paragraph{Kind-presence agreement does not imply rank-order agreement.}
The Cohen's $\kappa$ reported in \S\ref{sec:results-kappa} establishes that three independent labellers agree on which divergence kinds fire in each session. It does not establish that they agree on the order the score puts those divergence points in, which is what the dashboard surfaces first to the user. Closing this gap would require a separate rater-rank study (engage raters to rank the divergence points within each session, compute Kendall $\tau$-b against the score's production ranking). This was out of scope for the project. The chapter's strongest claim from $\kappa$ alone is that the score's kind labels match what the rater identifies, but the ranking that orders those labels is not externally validated.

\paragraph{Cleanliness sub-weights are uniform $1.0$ by Laplace's principle of insufficient reason.}
The six sub-signals that feed the cleanliness sub-score (\S\ref{sec:methodology-cleanliness}) are weighted equally because no published calibration data exists for weighting these signals across refactoring trajectories. The sensitivity sweep shows the cleanliness sub-weights have no effect on the top of the divergence-point ranking (Table~\ref{tab:results-sensitivity-knobs}: zero top-$1$ disruptions across all six sub-knobs), so the uniform choice is defensible in practice on the current set of sessions, but it is not a positive calibration result.

\paragraph{Two batching primitives are heuristic and not sensitivity-tested.}
The score formula and the divergence-point detector share two batching constants: \texttt{min\_commit\_gap}~$=6$ (the green-refactor checkpoint count at which a \texttt{COMMIT\_GAP} divergence point fires and the score formula applies the commit-gap penalty), and the $60$-second composite-window boundary that bundles refactoring steps into batches for \texttt{TESTS\_SKIPPED} and \texttt{ORDERING} detection. Both come from Murphy-Hill et al.'s 2009 study of how developers actually refactor~\cite{murphyhill2009howrefactor}, and neither is sensitivity-tested directly. The sensitivity sweep perturbs the weights on these terms but not the thresholds themselves. A different typing speed (slower or faster than the participants in this study) would group steps differently and could produce a different mix of fired kinds.

\paragraph{The production weight vector is hand-picked within a literature-grounded ordering.}
The exact production weights (\S\ref{sec:methodology-weights}, Table~\ref{tab:methodology-weights}) sit within the severity ordering the literature supports ($W_b > W_{st} > W_{cg}$ etc.), but no calibration step pins them to those specific numbers rather than any other vector inside the same ordering. The robustness experiments establish that the formula sits in a locally stable region around this vector; they do not establish that no other defensible vector exists in the same region.

\paragraph{Which terms carry the ranking varies by session type.}
The ablation analysis on the injection sessions identifies \texttt{manualIde}, \texttt{broken} and \texttt{length} as the dominant rank-carriers (Table~\ref{tab:results-ablation-loo}). On the user-study sessions the dominant carrier is \texttt{commitGap} instead (Table~\ref{tab:results-ablation-user-loo}). The formula's effective parameter dimension therefore depends on what kind of session is being scored. This connects to the gain-stripped finding in \S\ref{sec:results-userstudy}: the cleanliness-gain term dominates the absolute score, but doesn't affect the ranking of divergence points, so the formula effectively has two modes (an outcome-side mode that drives the absolute level and a process-side mode that drives the ranking). Whether a weight matters depends on which mode you care about—the absolute score or the ranking.

\paragraph{The reorder dependency analysis is deliberately coarse, and anchors are content-addressed.}
The dependency analyser that decides which refactoring steps can be safely reordered uses two design choices that affect the ORDERING recall reported in \S\ref{sec:results-divergence}. First, \texttt{Extract*} and \texttt{InlineMethod} specs are modelled optimistically: two extracts on the same host method, or two inlines of distinct methods on the same class, are not auto-conflicted, on the grounds that the downstream AST-equivalence check will reject any reorderings that conflict in practice. Second, the four specs that rewrite call sites of a named method (\texttt{RenameMethod}, \texttt{ChangeMethodSignature}, \texttt{ParameterizeVariable}, \texttt{ParameterizeAttribute}) are modelled coarsely: each writes the entire declaring class, which chains every later operation on the same class after it by construction, even when the operations would have been genuinely independent. Other rename specs (\texttt{RenameClass}, \texttt{RenameField}, \texttt{RenameLocalVariable}) only consume and produce the specific named entity rather than writing the whole class. On top of this, the entities that anchor each refactoring (a specific region of code, a specific local declaration) are identified by AST subtree hashes, so an identifier rename inside an anchor's region between two reorderable steps invalidates the second step's anchor. The optimistic model over-enumerates but the AST-equivalence check filters out the resulting false positives; the coarse model under-enumerates and removes some legitimate independence by construction. The $0.36$ ORDERING recall number reflects these two design choices. The headline §\ref{sec:results-headline} finding (``reordering rarely improves the score under the production formula'') is robust to them, because that finding follows from commuting refactorings producing identical end states under the formula, which does not depend on how the dependency analyser decides what commutes.

\paragraph{Cleanliness sub-signals are clamped to the user's range on alternative trajectories.}
Alt-trajectory cleanliness sub-signals are min-max normalised against the main (user) trajectory's observed range, then clamped to $[0, 1]$. An alternative whose intermediate checkpoints sit outside the user's range is masked at the boundary. The chapter mentions the clamp in \S\ref{sec:methodology-cleanliness} but does not flag it as a threat there; it is named here for completeness. The clamp does not affect the divergence-point magnitudes the chapter reports because the validator requires every accepted alternative to reach the same terminal AST as the user's trajectory, so alt and user share the same terminal-state cleanliness by construction; the trajectory-final $J$ computed from $\Delta C = C(s_T) - C(s_0)$ is identical on both. The clamp only changes how alt \emph{intermediate} checkpoints are displayed on the dashboard's cleanliness chart, which has no downstream effect on the scoring numbers.

\section{External validity}
\label{sec:threats-external}

\paragraph{The injection sessions are scoped exercises, not naturalistic sessions.}
The $45$ sessions in the primary test set were recorded by the author against a scripted playbook that injects one specific bad behaviour per session. They are scoped exercises rather than naturalistic refactoring activity; precision and recall claims drawn from this corpus are conditional on that controlled-injection structure, not on the variety of session shapes a free-form developer would produce in practice.

\paragraph{The user study has two participants and twelve sessions.}
This is the smallest meaningful sample for a multi-recorder study. The chapter shows each participant's trajectory and divergence-point counts, but doesn't report statistical tests, confidence intervals, or claims like ``the median participant improved on metric $X$''. The improvement in procedural discipline visible in the gain-stripped trajectory is real and matches what the feedback is designed to produce, but it's not quite enough proof that the feedback caused the improvement. We can't confidently separate the effect of feedback from the effect of becoming familiar with the codebase—both increase across the six sessions.

\paragraph{The agent comparison is a single agent over six sessions.}
This comparison uses a single agent (Claude Code, model \texttt{claude-opus-4-7}, tested on 2026-05-21)The comparison portrays the detector's behaviour on this particular coding agent rather than a generalisable claim about coding agents. A multi-agent extension is named as future work in \S\ref{sec:threats-out-of-scope}.

\paragraph{All sessions are single-author IntelliJ sessions on a Gradle Java project.}
The detector's behaviour on team pull-request-driven workflows, on other IDEs, or on other languages is not studied. The plugin's instrumentation is IntelliJ-specific: it uses the IDE's PSI and VFS hooks to capture refactoring events, which have no direct equivalent in VSCode or Xcode, so a port would require re-implementing the event-capture layer rather than just retargeting a build.

\paragraph{Refactoring kinds the synthesiser cannot handle are excluded by construction.}
The \texttt{IDE\_REPLAY} synthesiser builds alternative trajectories by replaying refactoring specs through Eclipse JDT. A user trajectory built entirely from refactoring operations outside JDT's supported set (or outside RefactoringMiner's detection capability, which feeds the synthesiser) would produce no \texttt{IDE\_REPLAY} divergence points. The detector cannot flag what it cannot synthesise an alternative for. This is a structural limitation of the detection design, not a finding specific to the sessions used in this thesis.

\section{Statistical conclusion validity}
\label{sec:threats-statistical}

Cohen's $\kappa$ in \S\ref{sec:results-kappa} is reported as a point estimate with raw cell counts. Confidence intervals are not reported because with only $45$ sessions per rater pair, they would be too wide to be useful and would overshadow the $\kappa$ value itself. Instead, the specific disagreement cases are listed (sessions 024 and 043 for \texttt{REWORK}; sessions 014, 015, 016, 039 for \texttt{HYGIENE}), which tells the reader more.

Kendall's $\tau$-b is brittle on small per-fixture rankings. A single pair swap on a three-point ranking moves $\tau$ by $0.333$. $\tau$ is reported on all three session sets, but the injection sessions' per-fixture rankings are short enough that the metric is dominated by the small-$N$ artefact; the sweep therefore reports top-$1$ hit rate alongside $\tau$ as a more stable surrogate, and the user-study sessions are treated as the canonical benchmark in \S\ref{sec:results-sensitivity}.

No multiple-comparison correction is applied across the weight $\times$ factor sweep ($12 \times 9 = 108$ cells per fixture). The headline result is a descriptive statistic — what fraction of cells preserve the top-$1$ recommendation — not a hypothesis test, so a correction would be appropriate only if individual cells were being interpreted as significance claims, which they are not. The multi-knob Monte Carlo uses a fixed seed and the $200$-sample size was chosen heuristically rather than from a power calculation; distribution shape (mean $\tau$, top-$N$ hit rate) is reported rather than a point estimate with confidence interval.

\section{Per-experiment threats}
\label{sec:threats-per-experiment}

\paragraph{The user study (\S\ref{sec:results-userstudy}).}
The external-validity limitations above ($n = 2$, no control) apply here too. The main concern with the gain-stripped score is what it can actually show us. The metrics in the gain-stripped score (commit cadence, skipped tests, manual-when-IDE rate) don't depend much on knowing the codebase, so improvement can't be explained by just getting more familiar with the fixture. But there are two other explanations we can't rule out. First is the Hawthorne effect: after seeing their dashboard in S1, a participant figures out what the system rewards—frequent commits, test runs, IDE actions—and adjusts their behavior to game the score. This looks like improvement, but it's behavior change to fit the metric, not actual skill improvement. We can't tell the two apart from the data. Second is learning the task format: all six sessions follow the same structure (a what-not-how prompt, a small change to make, look at the dashboard), so participants get better at the format itself, not at refactoring. We can't separate either confound from the study design, and we don't claim we can.

\paragraph{The agent comparison (\S\ref{sec:results-userstudy-agent}).}
The agent has no IDE-refactor surface available to it: Claude Code edits files via text tool calls, not through IntelliJ's Refactor menu, so every structural refactoring the agent performs is classified as manual by the detector. The \texttt{IDE\_REPLAY} count is therefore structurally bounded above zero whenever the agent performs any structural refactoring at all. This is a genuine measurement limitation, not a finding about the agent's capability. The agent itself acknowledges this in its S6 reflection (Thread 3 of \S\ref{sec:results-userstudy-agent}). The behavioral changes described in \S\ref{sec:results-userstudy-agent} are observational only ($n = 1$ agent, no control). We didn't run the agent without feedback, so while the data is consistent with ``feedback caused the change,'' we can't prove it.

\paragraph{The rater study (\S\ref{sec:results-kappa}).}
Three labellers including the author. The chapter reports pairwise $\kappa$ so author-vs-rater values are visible alongside the rater-vs-rater pair, which makes author self-bias detectable rather than hidden. The two participant raters are the same two participants who later performed the user-study sessions, so familiarity with the broader project is not zero; the labels were assigned before any participant performed a user-study session, however, so the labelling cannot have been biased by what the participants later did. Two of the four kinds (\texttt{REWORK} and \texttt{HYGIENE}) show $\kappa < 1.0$ with specific disagreement structure named in \S\ref{sec:results-kappa}; reconciliation was deliberately not applied to inflate $\kappa$, since the disagreements are themselves a finding about where the kind boundaries are fuzziest.

\section{Implementation threats}
\label{sec:threats-implementation}

The wrap-and-patch layer of \S\ref{sec:arch-wrap-patch} closes residual gaps between IntelliJ and Eclipse JDT for accepted reorder windows but is not exhaustive: windows where the gap cannot be patched are marked \texttt{ast\_diverged} and excluded from reordering.

\section{Out of scope}
\label{sec:threats-out-of-scope}

The following are explicitly outside the scope of this thesis. The chapters do not claim to address them.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multi-agent agent comparison.} Only Claude Code was run, with one model version on one date. A multi-agent or longitudinal agent study is named as future work in Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion}.
    \item \textbf{Longitudinal validation against external code-quality outcomes.} No outcome dataset linking refactoring-trajectory signals to downstream maintenance cost was available and we deemed constructing one as outside the scope of this thesis.
    \item \textbf{Cross-IDE and cross-language validation.} The plugin is IntelliJ-specific and the refactoring engine is JDT-specific (Java). Ports to other IDEs or languages are named as future work and not attempted.
    \item \textbf{Production-weight refitting against an outcome dataset.} See the outcome-dataset point above.
    \item \textbf{Real-time, live-feedback dashboard mode.} The dashboard is a session-replay view of completed analysis reports. Live feedback during refactoring is flagged in \S\ref{sec:results-userstudy} as a natural next step but is not implemented.
    \item \textbf{Rater-rank validation.} As named in \S\ref{sec:threats-internal}, the $\kappa$ study validates kind-presence agreement, not rank-order agreement. A Kendall $\tau$-b study on per-session rankings is the natural follow-up and is not done here.
\end{itemize}
